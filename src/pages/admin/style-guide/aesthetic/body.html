Design Aesthetic

The design direction for the Kixx admin panel.

This brief sets the design direction for the Kixx admin panel: Modern Humanist Minimalism. The goal is a digital environment that feels like a “Global Digital Park” — highly organised, open to everyone, and teeming with human expression. The design stays “invisible” enough to let the content shine, yet “bold” enough to signal quality and trust.

Everything below is intent, not implementation. The Colors, Typography, and component pages are where these principles harden into tokens and classes; this page is the why behind them.

Core design pillars

Three commitments the whole system answers to. Every smaller decision — a radius, a weight, a margin — should trace back to one of them.

Radical clarity

Information is tiered with an aggressive hierarchy. A user should never wonder where to find a resource or its documentation.

Open vitality

Avoid the “stuffy” feel of traditional open-source tooling. High-energy color and bold layout keep the surface alive without shouting.

Structural integrity

A clean, modular grid reflects the stability of the framework. It should feel dependable and orderly — softened corners and generous spacing keep it from turning rigid or cold.

Visual language

How the pillars show up on screen: type, color, icons, and illustration.

Typography

  • Primary typeface — a geometric or humanist sans-serif
  • Styling — heavy weights for hero headers to create impact; generous line-height (1.5+) for body copy to keep it accessible and breathable.
  • Direction — typography is the primary design element. Denote importance with scale, not decoration.

Color palette

  • Anchor colors — professional black and white provide the foundation.
  • Action palette — high-contrast accents (vibrant blue, magenta, and soft yellow).
  • Application — large blocks of color mark section breaks, giving long-form content a modular rhythm.

Iconography

Icons are plain, legible, and functional. A slight rounded quality is welcome, but never whimsical — use icons to clarify structure, not to decorate every line.

Illustration

Illustration is small, simple, and diagrammatic. It should feel like it came from a thoughtful manual, not a brand-agency moodboard.

Good directions

  • Simple line drawings.
  • Small object illustrations.
  • Browser-window sketches.
  • Tool-like icons.
  • Notebook-style diagrams.
  • Practical visual metaphors.

Avoid

  • Glossy 3D artifacts.
  • AI-generated people.
  • Corporate character illustrations.
  • Fantasy landscapes.
  • Aggressive retro pixel art.
  • Overly cute cartoons.

UI & interaction

The feel of the controls themselves — shapes, lines, and space.

  • Buttons — flat, fully-rounded “pill” shapes with bold labels. The rounded form is a signature of the system: friendly and approachable, never sharp. High-contrast hover states (e.g. magenta to black) give clear feedback.
  • Corners & radius — soften shapes with a consistent corner radius. Buttons and chips are full pills; cards, inputs, and surfaces carry a gentle, even radius. Rounding is a core part of the visual language, not an afterthought — define a small radius scale and apply it everywhere a corner meets, so nothing reads as hard-edged.
  • Borders & lines — thin, 1px lines separate grid modules. This keeps the surface clean and modular — structure you can read at a glance, without feeling severe.
  • Space — embrace macro white space. Large margins between sections reduce cognitive load and make dense information feel manageable.
  • Forms & inputs — input areas feel plain and trustworthy: easy to read, easy to tap on mobile, and visually obvious.

The tone

UI elements should communicate that the user is interacting with a community project, not entering a conversion funnel.

Functional requirements

The non-negotiables every screen has to honour, regardless of styling.

  • Responsiveness — mobile-first design. The modular grid must be fluid; on mobile, collage elements stack into a clean, vertical stream of information.
  • Dark theme — ship both a dark and a light theme. Follow the system default, but provide a sticky user switch.
  • Accessibility — color-contrast ratios should meet WCAG AA.

Inclusivity

The admin panel should avoid the gendered defaults common in developer tooling, without overcorrecting into something that feels soft or infantilising.

Steer clear of:

  • Aggressive hacker aesthetics.
  • Militarised language in visuals.
  • Dark-only terminal culture.
  • Hyper-competitive startup energy.
  • Overly masculine color and imagery conventions.

The design should feel equally appropriate for a hobbyist, teacher, small-business owner, scientist, artist, nonprofit operator, or programmer.

The goal

Welcoming competence. Present enough to feel capable and trustworthy, warm enough that nobody feels like they wandered into the wrong room.
