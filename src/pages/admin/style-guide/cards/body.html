Cards

The bordered surface primitive the rest of the UI sits on.

A card is a flat, hairline-bordered panel — the structural building block for dashboards, settings groups, and list modules. Softly rounded corners (--radius-md), a 1px rule, a flat fill: clean and modular, not decorative.

Because it's built entirely from semantic tokens — --color-rule for the border, --color-bg for the fill, and --color-ink-primary / --color-ink-secondary for accent variants — a card is correct in both light and dark with no extra work. It's a small, concrete demonstration of the color contract.

When to use: group related content into a distinct surface. When you only need to space things out, reach for .flow or a layout primitive instead — a card is a surface, not a spacer.

Anatomy

Four parts, with the accent border optional. The default card is just a surface, a hairline border, and padding.

Notification preferences

A surface with a hairline border, built-in padding, and an optional accent border in a brand ink.

  1. Surface — the flat fill (--color-bg); the card sits straight on the page, no shadow.
  2. Hairline border — a 1px rule on all four sides (--color-rule).
  3. Padding — built-in breathing room (--card-padding); removed by .card--flush.
  4. Accent border — optional; recolours the whole border in the primary or secondary accent and steps it up to --accent-border-width (2px), via .card--accent-primary / .card--accent-secondary.

Variants

A base class plus optional, composable modifiers. Accent borders come in primary and secondary only.

  • .card — the bordered surface, padded by default. Drop in any content.
  • .card--flush — removes the padding when the card is split into strips that pad themselves. The type specimens in the Typography section are flush cards.
  • .card--accent-primary — swaps the hairline for a full 2px border in the primary ink.
  • .card--accent-secondary — swaps the hairline for a full 2px border in the secondary ink.
  • .card--sunken — a recessed fill (--color-surface-sunken) for nested or read-only panels.

Default card

A padded surface with a hairline border. This is the default — reach for it first.

Primary accented card

The same surface with its full border recoloured in the primary ink.

Secondary accented card

A secondary accent border for a parallel, non-tertiary emphasis.

Sunken card

A recessed fill for nested or read-only panels, drawn from --color-surface-sunken.

Usage

Use a sectioning element (article / section) when the card is a content region; a plain div when it's purely visual.

<article class="card flow">
<h3 class="type-h3">Card title</h3>
<p class="type-body-sm">Any content goes here.</p>
</article>

<!-- Primary accent border -->
<article class="card card--accent-primary">…</article>

<!-- Secondary accent border -->
<article class="card card--accent-secondary">…</article>

<!-- Recessed surface for a nested panel -->
<article class="card card--sunken">…</article>

Accessibility

  • A card is a presentational surface — it adds no semantics. Put the real structure inside it (a heading, list, or landmark) so assistive tech still gets an outline.
  • Keep DOM order logical; the border groups content visually but doesn't change reading order.
  • Don't rely on the hairline alone to signal grouping — it uses --color-rule (maximum contrast) for exactly this reason. Keep it.
  • If a whole card is one target, wrap its content in a single <a> / <button> rather than nesting interactive elements, and keep a visible focus style.

Guidelines

Do

  • Reach for the default .card first; add a modifier only when the surface needs it.
  • Compose modifiers (.card--flush.card--accent-secondary) instead of writing a new card class.
  • Put real semantic structure inside; the card is only the surface.
  • Use .card--sunken for an inner panel instead of nesting full cards.

Don't

  • Add drop shadows or faux elevation — the system stays flat; depth comes from the hairline border, not shadow.
  • Hardcode a corner radius — the rounding comes from --radius-md; keep it consistent across surfaces.
  • Nest bordered cards more than one level deep; the rules start to fight.
  • Use a card just to create space — that's the job of .flow and the layout primitives.
  • Hardcode the border, fill, or accent; they come from the card modifiers and color tokens.
  • Add a tertiary card accent; cards support primary and secondary accent borders only.
